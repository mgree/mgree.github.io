Student Research

Michael Greenberg

I want to explicitly set out the terms for doing research with me as an undergraduate at one of the 5C colleges.

The idea for this (and much of the content!) are taken directly from Norman Ramsey, who has his own guide for students. Like Norman's guide, this is a work in progress.

Working together

Research is hard work, but we cannot lose sight of the fact that working together is a relationship, which requires work—like any other.

Our research relationship faces many challenges: power, knowledge, and experience are imbalanced; our goals may not always align; research is hard, full of dead ends and wrong answers.

I value honesty, candor, kindness, and integrity very highly. I also set a high bar in research. The more we align in these values, the easier the working relationship will be.

Goals

I want to do good, novel research, publishing good papers in good venues. I also want you to learn and succeed!

You want to do research, possibly for many reasons:

  • To find out what research in computer science is about
  • To solve a problem that no one has solved
  • To sharpen your computer science skills

It's important that I know up front what your goals are. If I don't know where you're looking to go, I can't help you get there. If we're not both headed in the same direction, things may or may not work out—I want to find that out early, not late!

What to do research on

I can give you a project or you can come to me with a project. If you bring me a project, we have to talk it through to make sure that it works for both of us—I only have expertise on/interest in certain topics.

If you're hoping to go into to a PhD program, working on publishable research now is a critical start. Most of my projects are planned to publish work along the way, so having me give you a project may be the safest bet.

The best research is done by people who care about what they're working on. Our work together will be at its best when we both care about the topic. We'll have to talk to figure it out.

How to do research

Working with me will mean meeting at least once a week to discuss what's been done so far and what to do next.

Keep notes. I don't care how, but keep them. I like pen and paper for meetings—an open computer tempts distraction—but I keep concrete TODO lists on my computer. I also keep an 'idea' list. If a meeting is going too fast for you to take notes, ask others to slow down.

When faced with any task, try to use your judgment: is the problem going to be solved by thirty minutes of searching online, by four hours of hacking, or by weeks of implementation? Quick things you can dive into, but complex things—coding that you can't immediately see the other side of—need planning.

Examples fuel intuition. One is okay, two is nice, three is a pattern. Decide on running examples early, and make sure things keep working. Take your examples from related work.

When you're not sure what to do next, speak up. I try to be fair with my time, but it's often the squeaky wheel that gets the grease. Sometimes writing up your question is all you need to do to answer it.

When in doubt, reply all.

Tools

Write documentation: plans, comments, READMEs, HOWTOs. Write tests: from tiny unit tests to larger examples, tests keep you honest. Automate your test suite so that a single command can tell you that your code still works. Even if you're doing theory work, write tests: you can use QuickCheck or PLT Redex or you can roll your own.

Use version control, specifically git. Public repositories on github are typically okay; if not, I will tell you to use a specific private repository at the beginning of the project.

Keep backups.

Publishing

Publishing isn't the end goal of research, but a published paper is a physical manifestation of concrete progress on a problem. Publishing is also one of the ways I'm evaluated as a professor; suffice to say, I try to publish good work frequently.

I don't expect most research to turn into publications. If something from your research is going to be published, you may be able to be a co-author. (Co-authoring a paper now will significantly boost your chances getting into good PhD programs.) We'll have to talk about it. I expect to do the bulk of paper submission, but not necessarily the bulk of the writing. Writing happens in LaTeX, which I can teach you.

Publishing in most venues in computer science means giving a talk. I enjoy giving talks, but would be delighted to let you speak instead.

Research during the semester and over the summer

There are two times to do research with me: during the semester and during the summer. The two are quite different! Work during the summer is full-time research: in your 10 weeks of SURP funding, you have no other job but research. During the semester, you're probably taking three other classes, each of which takes eight to twelve hours of your time every week. If you're working as an RA, you'll get paid according to how much you work. If you're working as part of an independent study, at end of a semester of research, I have to give you a grade!

There are two factors in determining that grade: process and outcome. Since I don't expect every semester of research to have an immediate outcome, I weigh process quite heavily. Even if the research isn't done, how have you done as a researcher?

  • Do you make progress between meetings?
  • When you're stuck, can you explain where you are and how you got there?
  • Can you explain your solutions to problems in plain English?
  • Is your implementation fit to be read by another person?

A good outcome is good, of course! There are two primary avenues for good outcomes:

  • Have you made tangible progress on the problem we're working on? Just failed attempts may not count for much, but showing why certain approaches won't work definitely does!
  • Have you learned a new skill?

Hopefully it's clear that there are multiple ways for me to assess your work at the end of the semester, and that my metric isn't "solving all of the problems".

Summer work is different. While I don't need to give you a grade at the end of working together, my measure of what counts for a good outcome are the same as those above. Since summer research with me is your only job, I tend to expect more of an outcome. I have some additional expectations on summer work:

  • Unless there's a reason otherwise, I expect summer work to last the full ten weeks allowable.
  • Attend weekly meetings with the group, giving feedback on others' work and occasionally presenting your own work.
  • Spend time in the lab every work day. To quote Norman Ramsey: part of your job is to be in the lab and talk with other people about your research.

Senior projects and theses

I am delighted to supervise senior projects and theses. If you would like me to supervise yours, come talk to me. My expectations for a senior project are similar to those of any other research over the semester... but note that projects and theses have even more deadlines built in than "end of the semester, time for grades"!

My responsibilities

I will provide funding: for summer research and R.A. salaries, but also for travel to conferences and workshops, materials for posters, etc. If you need something for research—textbooks, software, hardware—do not be afraid to ask.

I will provide expertise and guidance on everything in the research process: the topic, the goals, the relevance, the approach, the bibliography, and of course the work itself.

If you need to learn a skill, I will either teach it to you or find you resources so you can learn it.

I will sign administrative forms you prepare; I will go to bat for you when the bureaucracy is behaving badly.

I will help you with your career:

  • If you're interested in academia, I can explain to you the structure of CS academia, from scientific, administrative, and economic points of view.
  • I will try to find career options in whatever field you set your eye on.
  • I will write you an honest letter of recommendation. If I can't honestly write a good recommendation, I will be up front about it.
  • I will nominate you for awards and fellowships (as appropriate).

Your responsibilities

Actually do the work. I expect that you will do something between meetings, on your own. I don't expect any particular level of success (though good outcomes are good!), but I expect, at a minimum, effort.

Respect my and your time. Decide early on how much time you can commit—during the semester, you'll have to decide whether to do a half or whole credit of research quite early. If you're working for pay, rather than credit, decide early on how many hours a week you have to commit. If you need to make a change, say so before it's a problem.

Ask for help when you need it. Don't ask for help before trying to figure it out yourself and with your peers.

Let me know what's going on. Are you going to be away? Are you feeling unsatisfied or stuck with the problem you're working on? I can't help if you don't let me know.

Know when your next meeting is; show up on time... or reschedule as soon as you know you can't make it.

Do the administrative work of making sure you're getting appropriate credit (payment, course credit) from Pomona. For summer students, make sure you have housing set up for your dates.

Give me advance notice when you need a letter of recommendation. Two weeks is the minimum; a month is ideal.
